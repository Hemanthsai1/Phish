What Is Phishing?

Phishing is the process of attempting to acquire sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, and credit card details by masquerading as a trustworthy entity using bulk email which tries to evade spam filters. Emails claiming to be from popular social web sites, banks, auction sites, or IT administrators are commonly used to lure the unsuspecting public. It's a form of criminally fraudulent social engineering.

Spear Phishing

Think of spear phishing as professional phishing. Classic phishing campaigns send mass emails to as many people as possible, but spear phishing is much more targeted. The hacker has either a certain individual(s) or organization they want to compromise and are after more valuable info than credit card data. They do research on the target in order to make the attack more personalized and increase their chances of success.

Email/Spam

Using the most common phishing technique, the same email is sent to millions of users with a request to fill in personal details. These details will be used by the phishers for their illegal activities. Most of the messages have an urgent note which requires the user to enter credentials to update account information, change details, or verify accounts. Sometimes, they may be asked to fill out a form to access a new service through a link which is provided in the email.

Web Based Delivery

Web based delivery is one of the most sophisticated phishing techniques. Also known as “man-in-the-middle,” the hacker is located in between the original website and the phishing system. The phisher traces details during a transaction between the legitimate website and the user. As the user continues to pass information, it is gathered by the phishers, without the user knowing about it.

Link Manipulation

Link manipulation is the technique in which the phisher sends a link to a fake website. When the user clicks on the deceptive link, it opens up the phisher’s website instead of the website mentioned in the link. Hovering the mouse over the link to view the actual address stops users from falling for link manipulation.

Keyloggers

Keyloggers refer to the malware used to identify inputs from the keyboard. The information is sent to the hackers who will decipher passwords and other types of information. To prevent key loggers from accessing personal information, secure websites provide options to use mouse clicks to make entries through the virtual keyboard.

Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing conducted via Short Message Service (SMS), a telephone-based text messaging service. A smishing text, for example, attempts to entice a victim into revealing personal information via a link that leads to a phishing website.

Ways to Find Out Phishing

1. Check the URL

Identifying phishing attacks involves checking the URL for validity: hover over it to verify the domain. Look for a padlock icon and ensure the URL starts with 'https://' or 'shttp://', indicating encryption with SSL. However, phishing sites increasingly use SSL, so remain cautious. Pay attention to address spelling; fraudsters mimic official sites but alter subtly, like replacing 'co.uk' with 'org' or 'O' with '0', e.g., www.yah00.org. Watch for extra characters or symbols absent in official addresses.

2. Assess the content within a site

Identifying a phishing website often involves assessing its quality compared to official sites. Official websites are meticulously crafted with sharp graphics, impeccable spelling, and polished experiences. In contrast, phishing sites may exhibit sub-standard quality despite brand similarities. Look for spelling mistakes, broken English, grammatical errors, or low-resolution images as red flags. Additionally, the absence of a "contact us" section is suspicious; official sites typically provide comprehensive contact details, including a postal address, phone number, email, and social media channels. If these details are missing, it's likely a phishing site.

3. Check who owns the website

All domains will have to register their web address so it's worth doing a WHOIS look-up to see who owns the website. This is a free service and will enable you to check who owns the website when it was created and will provide contact details for the site owner. Suspicions should be raised if the website has been active for less than a year or if you think you're on the website of a leading brand, but the web address is registered to an individual in another country. If this is the case, it is more likely a phishing attack.

4. Presence of @ or -(Hyphen) symbol and Number of dots in Hostname in URL

If @ symbol present in URL then the feature is set to 1 else set to 0. Phishers add special symbol @ in the URL leads the browser to ignore everything preceding the “@” symbol and the real address often follows the “@” symbol.Phishing URLs have many dots in URL. For example http://shop.fun.amazon.phishing.com, in this URL phishing.com is an actual domain name, whereas use of “amazon” word is to trick users to click on it. Average number of dots in benign URLs.Phishers add dash symbol (-) to the domain name so that users feel that they are dealing with a legitimate webpage.
